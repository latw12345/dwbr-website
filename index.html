Chapter I United States 2010 — 2026

Sixteen jurisdictions, sixteen years, and a country slowly drawing itself in.

Excluded from federal labor law since 1935, domestic workers have spent nearly a century fighting for protections the rest of the workforce took for granted. What follows is what sixteen years of organizing has put back on the page: who passed, what they guaranteed, and how the map filled in.

02 Jurisdiction by jurisdiction

Sixteen laws, what each guaranteed, and when it was written.

From New York's 2010 first-in-the-nation law to Washington's 2026 passage, each jurisdiction built on what the last had put in writing.

New York 2010 +
Coverage
Nannies, housecleaners, home attendants working ≥40 hrs/week (≥44 for live-in workers)
Key provisions
Overtime (1.5×) after 40 hrs, 1 rest day/week, 3 paid days off after 1 year, harassment protections under Human Rights Law
First in the nation. It became the legislative template every state that followed adapted.
California 2013 +
Coverage
Personal attendants (nannies, caregivers for children or adults with disabilities), live-in and live-out
Key provisions
Overtime (1.5×) after 9 hrs/day or 45 hrs/week; made permanent via SB 1015 in 2016 after initial sunset clause
First state to extend overtime specifically to personal attendants, a category previously exempted under federal law.
Hawaii 2013 +
Coverage
Live-in and live-out domestic service workers; excludes babysitters working fewer than 20 hrs/week
Key provisions
Minimum wage, overtime after 40 hrs/week, comprehensive anti-discrimination (race, sex, age, disability, sexual orientation, ancestry)
Second state in the nation; brought domestic workers formally under the state's employer–employee framework for the first time.
Massachusetts 2014 +
Coverage
Nannies, housekeepers, caregivers, companions; workers' compensation required at ≥16 hrs/week
Key provisions
24 hrs rest/week, 48 hrs rest/month, parental leave protections, anti-harassment and discrimination, retaliation protections
Unusually strong rest-period and parental leave provisions, rare among DWBR laws at the time.
Connecticut 2015 +
Coverage
Domestic workers in childcare, housekeeping, caregiving, cooking; excludes casual babysitters and state-funded personal care attendants
Key provisions
Written employment info required at hire, paid leave (72–120 hrs/year), severance for wrongful termination, advance termination notice
Extended anti-discrimination protections to all household employers regardless of size; most state laws exempt small employers.
Oregon 2015 +
Coverage
Nannies, housecleaners, maids; excludes independent contractors, senior companions, and casual babysitters
Key provisions
24 consecutive hrs rest/week, 8 hrs rest per 24-hr period, adequate sleep conditions for live-in workers, 3 paid personal days/year (after 1 yr, ≥30 hrs/week)
Unique emphasis on sleep and rest protections, the most specific live-in worker rest provisions of any state law.
Illinois 2016 +
Coverage
Nannies, housecleaners, caregivers working ≥8 hrs/week for one employer; excludes family members
Key provisions
Anti-discrimination under Human Rights Act (including military service status), 24 hrs rest/week for workers ≥20 hrs/week, 20-min break per 7.5-hr shift
One of few state DWBRs to list military service as an explicitly protected characteristic.
Nevada 2017 +
Coverage
Domestic workers in private households; different provisions for live-in vs. live-out workers
Key provisions
Written employment agreements, minimum wage, overtime (after 8 hrs/day or 40 hrs/week), 1 full day off/week, privacy protections (no monitoring in bathrooms or bedrooms), lodging deductions capped at 5× minimum wage
Strongest privacy provisions in any state DWBR, explicitly prohibiting employer monitoring in workers' private spaces.
Seattle City · 2018 +
Coverage
All domestic workers within city limits (including part-time, live-in, and independent contractors), regardless of immigration status
Key provisions
Seattle minimum wage, 30-min meal break (5+ hrs), 10-min paid rest break (4+ hrs), document retention prohibited (passports, SSNs), immigration-related retaliation banned
First city DWBR in the nation. It became the organizing model Washington would follow eight years later.
New Mexico 2019 +
Coverage
Domestic service and home care workers previously excluded from state minimum wage protections
Key provisions
Minimum wage, overtime protections, wage enforcement by Dept. of Workforce Solutions, right to recover unpaid wages and damages
Closed the historical loophole that had excluded domestic workers from the state's minimum wage law entirely.
Philadelphia City · 2020 +
Coverage
Domestic workers (nannies, housecleaners, caregivers) working ≥5 hrs/month; covers workers regardless of immigration status
Key provisions
Written employment agreements, paid leave accrued across multiple employers, 2-week termination notice (4 weeks for live-in workers)
First ordinance to allow paid leave to accrue across multiple employers, addressing how domestic work is actually structured.
Virginia 2021 +
Coverage
Nannies, housecleaners, home care workers statewide; approx. 60,000 workers previously without any legal protections
Key provisions
Minimum wage, anti-discrimination and harassment protections, privacy protections for live-in workers, right to a safe workplace
First Southern state to pass a DWBR; workers previously had no legal recourse for sexual harassment or pregnancy discrimination.
District of Columbia City · 2022 +
Coverage
Housekeepers, nannies, home health aides in the District; approx. 9,000 workers previously excluded from the DC Human Rights Act
Key provisions
DC Human Rights Act protections (effective Oct. 2023), written work agreements required, domestic worker outreach and education program established
Reversed the DC Human Rights Act's historical exclusion of domestic workers, a direct legacy of the exclusions written into federal law in 1938.
Rhode Island 2024 +
Coverage
Domestic service workers in private homes (nannies, housecleaners, home care aides) previously exempted from the state minimum wage law
Key provisions
Minimum wage, overtime (1.5×) after 40 hrs/week, enforcement by state labor department
Eliminated the longstanding exemption of domestic workers from Rhode Island's minimum wage statute.
New Jersey 2024 +
Coverage
Domestic workers (childcare, housecleaning, elderly and disability care); excludes workers with fewer than 5 hrs/month; covers all workers regardless of immigration status
Key provisions
Workers' compensation, unemployment insurance, temporary disability and family leave insurance, NJ Law Against Discrimination protections, minimum wage and overtime
Most comprehensive inclusion of domestic workers in state insurance and benefits systems of any DWBR to date.
Washington 2026 +
Coverage
Nannies, housecleaners, home care aides, personal attendants working ≥4 hrs/month, one of the lowest coverage thresholds of any state
Key provisions
Minimum wage, overtime (1.5×) after 40 hrs/week, written employment agreements, advance termination notice, privacy protections (no monitoring in bathrooms or bedrooms); effective July 1, 2027
The thirteenth state, and the culmination of Seattle's eight-year proving ground. See Chapter III for the full legislative timeline.
03 Trends

What sixteen years of lawmaking reveals.

The movement started on the coasts and spread inward. The provisions it won grew more consistent with every bill, as each state borrowed what had survived legal challenge in the last.

Nine of the thirteen states passed their DWBRs in the first decade (2010–2019), averaging nearly one per year. The pace slowed slightly in the years since, with four more states following between 2021 and 2026, but the geographic reach widened: Virginia became the first Southern state in 2021, and Washington completed the Pacific Coast in 2026. Three cities (Seattle, Philadelphia, and the District of Columbia) also enacted their own ordinances; Seattle's preceded and directly shaped Washington's statewide law.

Across all sixteen jurisdictions, minimum wage coverage is nearly universal. Overtime, anti-harassment, and rest-break provisions are common at the state level; written employment agreements and paid leave are less consistent, with cities often filling gaps that state laws leave open.

04 Why it matters

Nearly ninety years in the making.

The FLSA exclusion of 1938 was not an oversight. It was a deliberate compromise. Reversing it, state by state, has taken most of a century.

The Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 created the federal floor for American work: minimum wage, overtime, the forty-hour week. It also, by deliberate compromise, left two groups out: agricultural workers and domestic workers. That carve-out has never been undone at the federal level.

What you are looking at, on the map above, is the slow work of putting it back, not in the District of Columbia, but across sixteen jurisdictions, statute by statute, over sixteen years of organizing.

Domestic workers explicitly carved out of federal labor protections. That gap remains today.
2010
First state law
New York's DWBR, the model the rest of the country would adapt.

Chapter II Seattle 2018

One city, one campaign, and the blueprint that held.

Eight years before Washington passed a statewide law, Seattle had to prove one was possible. Eighteen months of coalition-building, sixty-three house meetings, and one unanimous council vote turned a city ordinance into the organizing model a state would spend six years trying to replicate.

01 The campaign

Eighteen months that made the case.

A coalition built in workers' homes, drafted in four languages, and tested by returning the bill to workers four times before it was filed.

  1. 01
    Spring 2017
    Coalition forms.
    Casa Latina, SEIU Local 775, and Working Washington pull together a worker-led steering committee. The group begins organizing house meetings across the city.
  2. 02
    2017–2018
    House meetings and bill drafting.
    Organizers run sessions in Spanish, Tagalog, Amharic, and English, cataloguing wage theft, isolation, and the contracts no one had ever been offered. The final bill goes back to workers four times before it is filed.
  3. 03
    July 2018
    City Council votes 9–0.
    Seattle passes the first city-level Domestic Workers Bill of Rights in the United States. It covers minimum wage, rest breaks, written agreements, document protections, and establishes the nation's first Domestic Worker Standards Board.
  4. 04
    2019
    Standards Board holds its first session.
    The Seattle Domestic Worker Standards Board, seated half with workers, half with employers, begins meeting. It is empowered to recommend wage floors and working conditions directly to the City Council.
02 The model

What the city did that no state had managed yet.

The campaign worked because it began where the work happened: in private homes, in workers' own languages, in neighborhoods the council had never mapped.

Domestic work is invisible by design: it happens behind closed doors, in private homes, on schedules no employer publishes. The Seattle campaign began by making that geography visible: mapping the council districts where workers actually lived, the neighborhoods they cleaned, and the commute lines connecting them.

House meetings replaced public hearings. Organizers ran sessions in Spanish, Tagalog, Amharic and English, cataloguing wage theft, isolation, and the contracts no one had ever been offered. The final bill went back to workers four times before it was filed.

03 The ordinance

What Seattle's 2018 ordinance put in writing.

Six areas of protection. Two of them had never appeared in any domestic worker law anywhere in the country before Seattle wrote them.

Minimum wage
Seattle minimum wage applies in full, with no subminimum rates, no tip credits, regardless of employment status or immigration status.
Rest & meal breaks
30-minute unpaid meal break after 5 consecutive hours; 10-minute paid rest break for every 4 hours worked. Live-in workers get 24 unpaid hours off if required to work 6+ days straight.
Written agreements
Employers must provide a written work agreement before work begins, stating pay, schedule, duties, and conditions. Available in the worker's language.
Document protections
Employers are prohibited from retaining workers' passports, Social Security cards, or immigration documents, a practice used to trap live-in workers. First ordinance in the country to codify this.
Anti-retaliation
Employers may not threaten, report, or penalize workers for asserting their rights, including threatening to contact immigration authorities. Retaliation is a civil violation enforceable by the city.
Standards Board
A first-of-its-kind body, seated half with workers, half with employers, empowered to recommend minimum standards on wages and working conditions to the City Council. Washington's 2026 law created the same model at state scale.

They didn't write a bill and bring it to us. They sat in our living rooms until we wrote the bill ourselves.

— Casa Latina member, on the 2018 Seattle ordinance

Chapter III Washington 2026

Two chambers, one signature, and a state finally writing itself in.

Ninety years after domestic workers were written out of federal labor law, Washington wrote them back in. What follows is the record: sixty days, two chambers, and eighteen moments of testimony, votes, and uncertain momentum, tracing the path from a January prefile to the Governor's signature on March 9, 2026.

02 The road here

Washington's DWBR didn't start in 2025.

The 2026 victory is the culmination of six years of organizing, two prior attempts, and Seattle's municipal groundwork.

  1. 01
    2018 · City ordinance
    Seattle passes the country's first municipal DWBR.
    The Seattle ordinance covers minimum wage, rest breaks, written agreements, and establishes a Standards Board. It becomes the proof-of-concept for statewide legislation.
  2. 02
    2020 · First state attempt
    A statewide bill is introduced, and dies in committee.
    HB 1565 clears the House Labor committee but stalls in Appropriations. Opposition focuses on cost to households and concerns about enforcement in private homes.
  3. 03
    2021–2024 · Coalition building
    Organizers regroup and expand the base.
    The coalition runs 200+ listening sessions statewide, recruits employer advocates, and drafts a revised bill with phased implementation and household cost caps.
  4. 04
    2025–2026 · Second attempt
    The bill is refiled, and this time, it passes.
    HB 1399 / SB 5205 moves through both chambers in 60 days. The House passes 57–39 on Feb 12; the Senate follows 28–20 on Feb 26. Both chambers exceed simple-majority thresholds.

How the map voted, district by district.

04 What changed

The same goal, six years apart.

The bill failed in 2020 and passed in 2026. The difference was not what the coalition wanted but what it was willing to change to get there.

2020 attempt · HB 1565

Died in committee

Coverage
Immediate statewide coverage: all domestic workers, no phase-in period
Cost provisions
No household cost caps or exemptions for low-income employers
Support coalition
Worker organizations + labor unions, with limited employer advocates
Outcome
Cleared House Labor, stalled in Appropriations. Never reached the floor.
2025–26 attempt · HB 1399

Passed both chambers

Coverage
Phased implementation: core protections 7/1/27, live-in worker provisions deferred to 2028 study
Cost provisions
Sliding-scale subsidies for low-income households; exemptions for households below 200% FPL
Support coalition
Worker orgs + labor + employer advocates (Hand in Hand, Care.com employers) + disability rights groups
Outcome
Passed House 57–39, Senate 28–20. Delivered to Governor 3/3/26. Awaiting signature.
